iPhone 12 Long-term Review: Function over Form

I was extremely let down when Apple decided against releasing the iPhone 12 series with the new iPad Air and the refreshed iPad (10.2” with 1st Gen Apple Pencil support). However, Apple was quick to announce their plans for their “Hi, Speed” event on October 13th. The event was very Apple like. Some vague numbers and graphs, some comparisons from last generations and the ultimate buzz word of 2020: 5G. As preorders went live, I ordered my iPhone 12 in Blue which arrived on the 29th after billion years’ worth of delays. (Thanks FedEx) So from October 29th to present day, I’ve been daily driving the iPhone 12; which equates to about 2½ months of usage and here are my thoughts.

Unboxing Experience

The iPhone 12 is the culmination of what Apple calls their constant attempts to move to a more environmentally friendly packaging and achieving carbon neutrality. How does any of this affect you? Well for starters, you don’t get a charging brick in the box anymore and the EarPods that Apple’s been shipping since 2012 have been removed as well. So, what do you get? The phone, a USB-C to Lightening (-_-) cable, some documentation with just one Apple sticker in a very slim and sleek box that I kinda like. For me personally, Apple’s argument of its users reusing accessories like the power brick from their old phones make sense. I have plenty of USB-A to Lightening cables which work with anything ranging from the dreadful 5W Apple power adapter (thankfully it’s gone for good) to USB ports on computers and USB-C power bricks that can fast charge the iPhone 12 series to up to 21W. As for the removal of EarPods, I made the switch to wireless audio a while ago and so it doesn’t bother me all that much.

The iPhone 12: First Impressions

Coming back to the phone itself, I really like the new squared chassis reminiscent of the iPhone 4 and 4S. It’s easier to have a firm grip compared to the concave Stainless Steel on the Pro series or the Aluminum from the 11 and the XR. At just 164g, the weight is evenly distributed and heft in hand is not overpowering like the infamous 225g brick, the iPhone 11 Pro Max. The iPhone 12 is a full 30g lighter than its predecessor ― iPhone 11 ― which I’m assuming came from the fact that the entire iPhone 12 line up now sports an OLED display (more on that in a bit). However, my biggest concern was the ~10% reduction in battery size i.e. from 3110mAh on the 11 to 2815mAh on the 12. (more on that later as well) Overall the design hasn’t changed a lot since the iPhone X from 2017 which means the display still has a notch to accommodate all the FaceID sensors and the bezels are kept relatively slim. The back is still free of FCC labels and looks exactly like the iPhone 11 (lol). This year, the iPhone 12 also received a spec bump in terms of water resistance which is now rated at up to a maximum depth of 6 meters up to 30 minutes. Buttons are very tactile and the mute switch is ever so useful. The glass sandwich design is as fragile as before……. except it isn’t. INTRODUCING CERAMIC SHIELD. Puns aside, developed in collaboration with Corning, Apple claims an impressive 2x more shatter resistance than the previous generation glass on the iPhone 11. What’s kind of a let down here is that there is no mention of any improvements per se on scratch resistance. It still scratches at a level 6 with deeper grooves at a level 7. Zach however, does note that the scratches are ever so slightly less visible especially the scratches from the level 6 pick so there’s that.

Another thing that’s cough cough new here is MagSafe confusion. Apple being Apple, presented the whole in way where it felt like they actually created brand-new and revolutionary technology (completely ignoring its prior presence on old MacBooks) to wirelessly charge your phone with drum roll the same Qi standard that’s been here for over half a decade now. Wireless charging speeds can go up to 15W which finally brings Apple in the same ballpark as say Samsung who have now standardized 15W wireless charging on their flagships for a while now. Unsurprisingly, the pad itself only works properly when connected to an Apple branded 21W USB-C power brick and physically the only real difference between this and any other wireless charger would be how the MagSafe charging pad uses magnets to align itself properly with the iPhone’s body and since the magnets are quite strong on this thing, it brings with itself the ability to stick a whole bunch of “MagSafe compatible” accessories like a wallet. And well it works and… that’s about it for MagSafe. For me, MagSafe is nothing but a glorified wireless charger that has failed to garner any attention from me.

Display

As mentioned earlier, the entire 12 line up now uses Super Retina XDR screens which is a fancy phrase for OLED. The key differences between the iPhone 12/12 mini’s OLED screen and the 12 Pro/Max’s OLED screen are the manufacturer; BOE vs. Samsung and the maximum brightness; 625 nits vs. 800 nits (typical) both displays however peak at 1200 nits when viewing HDR10+ content. As with OLED screens, the screen has excellent contrast with deep inky blacks and bright but true to life color reproduction. There is also 100% coverage of the P3 color gamut and usual support for True Tone and Night Shift. Another huge improvement aside from the move to OLED from IPS LCD is the bump in resolution. On the 12, we are finally above the abysmal 1792 x 828 weird wonky barely HD resolution at 326 ppi to 2532 x 1170 resolution at 460 ppi which a step above Full HD (1080p) but slightly below Quad HD (1440p).

Specifications aside, the display is a pleasure to look at. From countless Call of Duty mobile games to rewatching The Office, the display gets bright and dim enough (minimum being just 1 nit!) where it poses no issues even under harsh sunlight or while reading something before going to bed. However, its not all perfect. Two of my major complaints would be the design; it seems a bit outdated now with the big notch straight from 2017 and fact being that we are still stuck at 60Hz. In 2020, no flagship should have a 60Hz screen yet here we are. It’s not a deal breaker for me (the move OLED made me happy enough I guess) but no one’s buying phones in a vacuum and in that regard, I can easily see people getting upset about the missing HFR displays.
